Setup Guide

Turn this template into your own website

This walks you through turning this template into your own live website, step by step. You don’t need to know how to code, and you don’t need a terminal — just a web browser.

You’ll do everything in two places: GitHub (where your website’s files live) and your web host (the company that actually serves your website to visitors — e.g. Bluehost). A few steps are unavoidably technical because that’s just what it takes to run your own independent site — but every step below tells you exactly what to click.

Before you start

What you’ll need

  • A GitHub account (free) — github.com/join if you don’t have one.
  • A PHP web hosting account with FTP access — e.g. Bluehost, HostGator, SiteGround. Any host that lets you host “PHP” or “static” sites works.
  • A domain name — usually bought through your host, or elsewhere and pointed at your host.
  • A Gmail account you’re willing to send contact-form emails from — a regular address works fine, you’re only creating a separate “app password” for this, not changing your real password.
01

Make your own copy of this template

  1. On this repository’s GitHub page, click the green “Use this template” button near the top right, then “Create a new repository.”
  2. Choose an owner (your GitHub account) and a name for your new repository — this can be anything, e.g. my-portfolio.
  3. Leave it set to Public (the CMS needs to read your content without you being signed in, so the repo can’t be Private).
  4. Click Create repository.

You now have your own, completely independent copy. Nothing you do next affects the original template.

02

Get your web host’s FTP details

Your web host will deploy your GitHub repository to your host account automatically every time you save something. To let that happen, you need three pieces of information from your host — usually found under a section called FTP Accounts or FTP/SFTP in your hosting control panel (cPanel):

What to copy down
FTP serveroften just your domain, or ftp.yourdomain.com
FTP usernamefrom your hosting control panel
FTP passwordfrom your hosting control panel

Write these down or keep the tab open — you’ll paste them into GitHub next.

03

Add your FTP details to GitHub

In your new repository on GitHub, go to Settings → Secrets and variables → Actions. You’ll see two tabs — add these:

Variables tab — “New repository variable”
NameValue
FTP_SERVERyour FTP server/hostname from Step 2
FTP_USERNAMEyour FTP username from Step 2
Secrets tab — “New repository secret”
NameValue
FTP_PASSWORDyour FTP password from Step 2

Secrets are hidden after saving — even from you — which is why the password goes there, while the server and username (not sensitive) go in Variables where you can see them again later.

04

Create a GitHub sign-in for your CMS

Your website’s editor (/admin/) uses “Sign in with GitHub” so only you (or people you invite) can edit it. This needs a one-time setup called an OAuth App.

  1. Go to github.com/settings/developers → OAuth Apps → New OAuth App.
  2. Fill in an application name (anything), and:
    Homepage URLhttps://yourdomain.com
    Callback URLhttps://yourdomain.com/oauth/callback.php
  3. Click Register application, then copy the Client ID.
  4. Click Generate a new client secret and copy it too — you won’t see it again after leaving the page.

Add both to your repository the same way as Step 3 (Settings → Secrets and variables → Actions → Secrets tab):

Secrets tab
NameValue
GH_OAUTH_CLIENT_IDthe Client ID you copied
GH_OAUTH_CLIENT_SECRETthe client secret you copied
05

Set up your contact form’s email

Your contact form sends messages through your own Gmail account. This creates a special “app password” (not your real Gmail password) and uploads one small file to your web host.

  1. Go to myaccount.google.com/apppasswords (you may need 2-Step Verification turned on first).
  2. Create an app password, and copy the 16-character value it gives you.
  3. In your GitHub repository, open smtp-config.sample.php and copy its contents.
  4. Using your host’s File Manager or an FTP program, create a new file called smtp-config.php in the same folder as contact.php, and paste in those contents.
  5. Edit the values you pasted — username and from to your Gmail address, password to the app password, to to your own inbox (a fallback; Step 7’s CMS setting takes priority).
  6. Save the file on your host.

This file is intentionally never part of your GitHub repository — it lives only on your web host, since it holds a real password.

06

Trigger your first deploy

Now that your credentials are in place, make GitHub build and deploy your site:

  1. In your repository, go to the Actions tab.
  2. Click the most recent workflow run (it likely shows a red × from before your credentials were set) → click Re-run all jobs in the top right.
  3. Wait a minute or two — a green checkmark means your site has been built and uploaded to your host.

If it’s your first time pointing this domain at your host, you may also need to update your domain’s DNS/nameservers — your host’s support docs will walk you through that.

Visit your domain — your new site should be live.

07

Start editing your site

Go to yourdomain.com/admin/ and click Sign in → Sign in with GitHub. Approve the popup — you’re in.

The dashboard has four page cards (Home, About, Contact, Design) and a Case studies section with two sample projects to start from.

Editing text or images
Open a page, click Edit, then click directly on any heading, paragraph, or photo to change it. Click Save — it deploys automatically in a couple of minutes.
Case studies
Open “Sample Case Study One/Two,” turn on Edit, and replace the placeholder text and images with your own work. Use + New case study to add more, or delete a sample one entirely.
Design panel
Set your name or upload a logo, pick fonts, adjust text sizes and letter-spacing for each of the four text styles (Display, Heading, Body, Caption), and set your site’s colors. A tick mark on each slider shows its default — click Reset to return to it.
Contact form
Set your notification email and subject line. Your form starts with Name, Email, Organization, Project, and Other Notes — rename any label, change a field’s type, mark it required, reorder it, or delete it, and edit dropdown options directly underneath. Add new fields with + Add field. The Email field can’t be deleted or retyped — the form needs exactly one to know who to reply to — but its label can still be renamed.

Every Save is one commit to your repository and triggers an automatic rebuild — check the Actions tab to watch it happen.

Troubleshooting

Why did my deploy fail in the Actions tab?
Click into the failed run to see which step failed. Most often it’s a typo in one of the Secrets/Variables from Steps 3–4 — double check the exact values against your host.
Why doesn’t “Sign in with GitHub” work?
Double-check the Homepage URL and Callback URL in your OAuth App (Step 4) exactly match your real domain, including https://.
Why aren’t contact form messages arriving?
Confirm smtp-config.php was uploaded to your host (it’s never in GitHub, so a fresh deploy can’t create it for you), and that the Gmail app password was copied correctly.
What if I change my mind about a Design setting?
Every style, color, and the Site Identity name/logo can be reset independently — nothing is permanent until you click Save.
